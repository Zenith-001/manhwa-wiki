History of Manhwa

Manhwa has a rich and complex history spanning over a century. From its earliest roots in newspaper comic strips during the Japanese colonial period, to the global digital phenomenon it is today, manhwa has continuously evolved alongside Korean society, politics, and technology. This page covers the full journey of Korean comics from the late 1800s to the present day.

Early Origins (Late 1800s ‐ 1900s)

The word manhwa shares the same Chinese characters as the Japanese word manga and the Chinese word manhua, all roughly translating to "impromptu sketches" or "whimsical pictures." The art form's earliest traces in Korea can be found in the late Joseon Dynasty, where satirical illustrations began appearing in printed media.

The first widely recognized Korean comic strip appeared in the newspaper Daehan Minbo on June 2, 1909. It featured political cartoons drawn by illustrator Lee Do-yeong, criticizing Japanese colonial influence over Korea. This marked the formal beginning of manhwa as a published art form.

During the Japanese colonial period (1910–1945), Korean artists were heavily influenced by Japanese illustration styles but also used the comic strip medium as a tool of subtle resistance and social commentary. Many publications were censored or shut down by colonial authorities, making manhwa a quietly defiant art form from its very beginnings.

Post-Liberation & Korean War Era (1945 ‐ 1950s)

With Korea's liberation from Japanese rule in 1945, manhwa artists regained creative freedom. A wave of new comic strips flooded Korean newspapers, reflecting the joy, chaos, and uncertainty of the post-colonial period. Political satire remained dominant, with cartoonists openly critiquing the shifting political landscape of the newly divided peninsula.

The Korean War (1950 ‐ 1953) deeply impacted all forms of art, including manhwa. During and after the war, comics served as both entertainment and a form of escapism. Children's comics and humor strips became popular as people sought relief from the devastation of the conflict.

One of the most important developments of this era was the rise of Manhwa-bang, small street stalls or rental shops where people could pay a small fee to read manhwa on-site. These stalls made comics accessible to the general public and helped establish manhwa as a mainstream form of entertainment in Korean culture.

The Golden Age of Print Manhwa (1960s ‐ 1990s)

The 1960s through the 1990s are widely considered the Golden Age of Print Manhwa. Manhwa magazines and dedicated publishing houses flourished. Genres diversified significantly, covering adventure, romance, martial arts, children's stories, and science fiction.

Some of the most beloved manhwa titles were published during this era. Artists like Kim Sung-hwan, creator of Gobau, became household names in Korea. Gobau ran for over 10,000 strips and became one of the longest-running comic strips in history, deeply integrating manhwa into Korean daily life.

This era was also marked by heavy government censorship. The authoritarian governments of Park Chung-hee and Chun Doo-hwan imposed strict content regulations, banning anything deemed politically subversive, violent, or morally inappropriate. Many artists self-censored or had their works pulled entirely.

By the 1980s and early 1990s, manhwa had grown into a full industry with dedicated magazines such as IQ Jump and Champ serializing multiple titles simultaneously, similar to Japanese manga anthologies.

The Digital Revolution & Birth of Webtoons (2000s)

The late 1990s and early 2000s brought a dramatic shift. South Korea became one of the most internet-connected countries in the world, with high-speed broadband reaching homes across the nation rapidly. This technological leap would completely transform manhwa.

In 2003, Naver launched its online comics platform, and Daum followed closely with its own webcomic service. Artists began publishing directly online, bypassing traditional print publishers entirely. This gave creators far greater freedom and direct access to readers.

The defining innovation of this era was the vertical scroll format. Unlike traditional print comics, webtoons were designed to be read by scrolling downward on a screen, with panels arranged in long vertical strips. This format was a uniquely Korean invention and would go on to influence digital comics worldwide.

Early landmark webtoons attracted millions of readers and proved that digital manhwa could be just as compelling as print. The free access model, supported by advertising revenue, made webtoons available to anyone with an internet connection.

The Global Expansion (2010s)

Throughout the 2010s, manhwa began making serious inroads into international markets. Naver Webtoon launched its global English-language platform in 2014, bringing thousands of Korean webtoons to readers in North America, Southeast Asia, and Europe with official translations and a free-to-read model.

Competing platforms like Kakao Webtoon, Lezhin Comics, and Tapas also expanded internationally, offering both free and premium content. Fan translation communities and scanlation groups had already been spreading manhwa to international readers for years before official platforms caught up.

Titles like Tower of God, The God of High School, and Noblesse became internationally recognized during this period, laying the groundwork for the explosion that was to come.

Manhwa in the Modern Era (2020s ‐ Present)

The 2020s marked manhwa's full arrival on the global stage. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated digital content consumption worldwide, and manhwa benefited enormously as platforms reported record numbers of new readers during lockdowns.

Solo Leveling became a worldwide phenomenon, regularly topping global manhwa charts and being discussed alongside the biggest manga titles. Its anime adaptation in 2024 brought manhwa to an entirely new audience of anime fans globally.

Korean dramas adapted from manhwa and webtoons, such as All of Us Are Dead and Itaewon Class, reached global audiences through Netflix, further cementing manhwa's cultural footprint. The broader Korean Wave (Hallyu) and global success of K-pop brought international audiences closer to all things Korean, including manhwa.

Today manhwa is a multi-billion dollar industry with hundreds of millions of readers worldwide, its own dedicated awards, and an ever-growing presence in mainstream global pop culture.
