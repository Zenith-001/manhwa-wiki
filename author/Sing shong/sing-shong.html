About

Sing Shong is the shared pen name of a married South Korean couple who co-authored Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint, one of the most celebrated Korean web novels and manhwa of the modern era. Sing is the husband and Shong is the wife — their real names have never been publicly disclosed. The couple are intensely private, having revealed in a Yen Press interview that despite the international reach of their work, Sing has only traveled to Japan once and Shong only to Taiwan once, remarking with characteristic humility that Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint has been to more countries than they have.

Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint was first published on January 6, 2018, on the Korean web novel platform Munpia, where it quickly became a phenomenon, accumulating over 26 million views on the platform alone by September 2019. The novel concluded on February 2, 2020. A webtoon adaptation created by Redice Studio began on Naver Webtoon in 2020 and remains ongoing. A film adaptation starring Ahn Hyo-seop and Lee Min-ho was released in South Korea on July 23, 2025, grossing $7.9 million worldwide as of October 2025. An anime adaptation has also been announced.

The pair have spoken openly about how Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint was born out of a creative slump following the completion of their previous work. Frustrated and uncertain what to write, they asked themselves what kind of story readers would truly want to see — and then realized they had not thought about stories purely as readers themselves in a long time. That shift in perspective became the emotional foundation for a novel whose central theme is the power of stories to help people survive. Prior to ORV, Sing Shong also wrote The World After the Fall, and ORV characters have made guest appearances in that work's side stories.

Writing Style & Themes

Sing Shong's writing is densely layered, emotionally sophisticated, and deeply concerned with the nature of stories themselves. Omniscient Reader's Viewpoint is meta-fictional at its core — a story about a man whose survival depends on his encyclopedic knowledge of a novel, and who must reckon with what it means to live inside a narrative. The duo drew inspiration from works in literary theory and philosophy, including Roland Barthes's A Lover's Discourse and Pierre Bayard's How to Talk About Books You Haven't Read, both of which influenced their exploration of the relationship between readers, writers, and the stories that shape them.

Thematically, their central message is one of quiet but profound hope — that stories can serve as lifelines for people enduring hardship. In their own words, they wanted to convey that salvation does not need to be grand, and that if a single story can help someone endure for a moment, then that is enough. The two write with a strong sense of individual voice for each character: Sing has said he most resembles Lee Gilyoung in personality, while Shong most resembles Han Sooyoung. Their writing is notable for its emotional restraint and the way it rewards patient, attentive readers who pick up on the thematic threads woven throughout the text.
