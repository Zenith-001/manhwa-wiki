What is Manhwa?

Manhwa (만화) is the term for Korean comics and print cartoons. The word itself simply means "comics" in Korean, but internationally it has come to specifically refer to comics created in South Korea. Just as Japan has manga and China has manhua, South Korea has developed its own distinct comic tradition that has grown into a globally recognised art form.

Manhwa covers an enormous range of genres — from action-packed fantasy and romance to horror, mystery, and slice-of-life stories. It is enjoyed by readers of all ages, both in Korea and across the world.

Where does it come from? Manhwa comes from South Korea. Its roots trace back to the early twentieth century, and it has evolved through decades of political change, technological shifts, and cultural growth to become one of Korea's most significant creative exports.

How is it different from Manga and Manhua?While manhwa, manga, and manhua are all forms of Asian comics, they each have distinct identities. Manga is Japanese and is traditionally read right-to-left. Manhua is Chinese and varies in format. Manhwa is Korean and is almost always read left-to-right, following the Western reading direction. In terms of art style, manhwa tends to feature detailed, expressive character designs with a heavy emphasis on colour — especially in its digital form.

The Webtoon FormatThe most significant innovation in manhwa's modern history is the webtoon — a digital comic format designed to be read on a smartphone or computer by scrolling vertically from top to bottom. Unlike traditional comics with fixed panel grids, webtoons use a long, continuous vertical strip that guides the reader seamlessly from scene to scene. This format was pioneered in South Korea in the early 2000s and has since become the dominant way manhwa is created and consumed worldwide.

Global Popularity Today, manhwa is read by tens of millions of people across the globe. Platforms like Naver Webtoon and Kakaopage have international versions serving readers in North America, Europe, Southeast Asia, and beyond. Several manhwa titles have been adapted into anime series and live-action dramas, bringing Korean comics into mainstream global entertainment.

History of Manhwa

Manhwa has a history stretching over a century, shaped by occupation, war, censorship, and eventually a digital revolution that took it to the entire world.

The earliest Korean comics appeared in the early 1900s, during the period of Japanese colonial rule (1910‐1945). These were primarily political cartoons published in newspapers, used as a form of social commentary and quiet resistance.

After Korea's liberation and the devastation of the Korean War (1950‐1953), manhwa became a popular form of cheap entertainment for a struggling population. Comic rental shops called manhwabang (만화방) became widespread, allowing readers to borrow comics for a small fee. This era produced some of Korea's most beloved classic characters.

The following decades saw manhwa grow as an industry, though it faced heavy government censorship during periods of authoritarian rule. Despite this, creators continued to produce influential works across a range of genres.

The true turning point came in the early 2000s with the rise of the internet. Korean web portals, most notably Naver and Daum, began hosting comics online for free. This gave birth to the webtoon format — vertical-scrolling, full-colour digital comics built for screen reading. Suddenly, any creator could publish directly to an audience of millions.

By the 2010s, platforms like Naver Webtoon and Kakaopage had launched international services, and manhwa began reaching readers far beyond Korea. Titles like Tower of God, The God of High School, and later Solo Leveling attracted massive global followings and sparked a wave of international interest in Korean comics.

Today, manhwa stands as one of the most dynamic and fast-growing comic industries in the world.

How Manhwa is Published

Manhwa is published in two main formats: traditional print and digital webtoon.

Print Manhwa Print manhwa is published in physical volumes, similar to manga tankōbon. These are sold in bookshops and online retailers, and often collect previously serialised chapters into compiled editions. Print manhwa was the dominant format through most of the twentieth century but has become less common as digital publishing has taken over.

Digital WebtoonsThe webtoon is the modern face of manhwa. Stories are published as individual episodes (also called chapters) on digital platforms, released on a regular schedule — most commonly once per week. Each episode is formatted as a long vertical image strip optimised for scrolling on a mobile device. Readers follow a series week by week, much like a television show.

Major Publishing Platforms

  • Naver Webtoon — The largest webtoon platform in the world, launched in South Korea in 2004. Available in multiple languages through its international service, now simply called Webtoon.
  • Kakaopage / Kakao Webtoon— A major Korean platform operated by Kakao Corp, home to many top-tier titles and a large paid content library.
  • Lezhin Comics — A premium platform known for mature and adult-rated content, with a strong international readership.
  • Tapas— An English-language platform popular with indie creators and translated manhwa.
  • Tappytoon — Specialises in officially licensed, translated Korean webtoons for international audiences.

Free vs Paid ContentMost platforms operate on a mixed model. New episodes are often free to read but are released on a delay. Readers who want early access can pay using the platform's currency system (Coins, Flowers, etc.) to unlock episodes immediately. Some series are entirely behind a paywall, while others are completely free. This model allows platforms to generate revenue while keeping content widely accessible.
